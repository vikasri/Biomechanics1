Statics (TEST)

Vectors are shown in bold.

  1. Force, Position and Moment Vectors in a Continuum Body
    In a cartesian coordinate system with origin \(O\) and unit vectors {\(\bm{\hat{e}}_x, \bm{\hat{e}}_y, \bm{\hat{e}}_z\)} \[\begin{aligned} \textrm{Position vector of a point P:} \quad \textbf{r} &= r_x\,\bm{\hat{e}}_x +r_y\,\bm{\hat{e}}_y +r_z\,\bm{\hat{e}}_z\\ \textrm{A force (vector) acting on P:} \quad \textbf{F} &= F_x\,\bm{\hat{e}}_x +F_y\,\bm{\hat{e}}_y +F_z\,\bm{\hat{e}}_z\\ \textrm{Moment due to force } \textbf{F} \textrm{ about O:} \quad \textbf{M}_O = \textbf{r}\times\textbf{F} &= {M_O}_x\,\bm{\hat{e}}_x+{M_O}_y\,\bm{\hat{e}}_y+{M_O}_z\,\bm{\hat{e}}_z \end{aligned}\] The magnitude of the moment about an axis due to a force is given by the force magnitude \(\times\) minimum distance between the axis and the force vector line (perpedicular distance).

  2. General Equilibrium Equations
    If multiple forces and moments act on a body represented by \(i = 1,2,3\)...
    Vector Form: \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \textbf{F}^{(i)}=\textbf{0}\] \[\sum_{i=1}^{p} {\textbf{M}_O}^{(i)}=\textbf{0}\] Scalar Form: \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} F_x^{(i)}=0, \quad \sum_{i=1}^{n} \;F_y^{(i)}=0, \quad \sum_{i=1}^{n} F_z^{(i)}=0\] \[\sum_{i=1}^{p} {M_O}_x^{(i)}=0,\quad\sum_{i=1}^{p} {M_O}_y^{(i)}=0,\quad \sum_{i=1}^{p} {M_O}_z^{(i)}=0\]

  3. Centroid \[\tilde{X}=\frac{\int \;xdv}{\int \; dv},\quad \tilde{Y}=\frac{\int \;ydv}{\int \; dv}, \quad \tilde{Z}=\frac{\int \;zdv}{\int \; dv}\]

  4. Center of Gravity
    Discrete Bodies System (each body is represented by subscript \(i\), where \(i = 1,2,3,\)...): \[\bar{X}=\frac{\sum\limits_{i} x_i\, w_i}{\sum\limits_{i} w_i},\quad \bar{Y}=\frac{\sum\limits_{i} y_i \, w_i}{\sum\limits_{i} w_i},\quad \bar{Z}=\frac{\sum\limits_{i} z_i\, w_i}{\sum\limits_{i} w_i}\] Continuum Body: \[\bar{X}=\frac{\int \;xdw}{\int \; dw},\quad \bar{Y}=\frac{\int \;ydw}{\int \; dw},\quad \bar{Z}=\frac{\int \;zdw}{\int \; dw}, \quad \textrm{where} \quad dw=\rho dV\]

  5. Dry Friction
    \[\textrm{Static: } \; F_f \leqslant \; \mu_s \, N, \qquad \textrm{Dynamic: } F_f = \; \mu_k \, N\]

  6. Gravitational Acceleration: \(g=9.8\; m/s^2\)

Simple/1D Small Strain, Stress, and Hooke’s Law

Consider simple (uniaxial) tension/compression (stress is applied in the axial direction, and there are no stresses in transverse (perpendicular to axial) directions). For shear, consider a force parallel to the surface. \(A_0, L_0, w_0\) are the original (relaxed) cross-sectional area, axial length (along loading direction), and width in the transverse direction, respectively. \(A, \Delta L, \Delta w\) are the deformed (loaded) body cross-sectional area, change in axial length, and change in transverse width, respectively.

Stress and Strain: \[\textrm{Stress} \quad \sigma = \frac{F}{A} \approx \frac{F}{A_0} , \qquad \textrm{Normal strain} \quad \epsilon =\frac{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup L}{L_0}\]

shear strain = change in angle of perpendicular lines = \(\gamma \; (\approx \tan \, \gamma)\)
Linear Elasticity: In the elastic limit (before material yield occurs).

  1. Hooke’s Law \[\begin{aligned} \textrm{Normal stress-strain relation:} \quad \sigma &= E \, \epsilon, \quad \textrm{(where $E$ is Elastic/Young's Modulus)} \\ \textrm{Shear stress-strain relation:} \quad \tau &= G \,\gamma \; (\textrm{OR} \; \sigma_{ij} = G \,\gamma_{ij}, \; \textrm{when } i\neq j) \quad \textrm{($G$ is Shear Modulus)} \end{aligned}\] Note: Tensorial strain \(\epsilon_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{ij}\)

  2. Poisson’s Ratio \[\nu=-\frac{\textrm{transverse strain}}{\textrm{ axial strain}}=-\frac{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup w/w_0}{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup L/L_0}\] Note: for incompressible materials (no volume change under deformation), \(\nu = \frac{1}{2}\)

  3. Strain Energy Density (Energy per Unit Volume) \[\begin{aligned} \textrm{Strain energy density (General): } \quad \mathcal{U} &=\int \sigma\,d\epsilon\\ \textrm{For \emph{linear elastic material}, $\mathcal{U}$ is \emph{elastic energy density stored}:} \quad \mathcal{U} &= \dfrac{1}{2}E\epsilon^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}\sigma \epsilon = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2E}\\ \textrm{Total energy stored for the entire volume:} \quad \mathbb{U} &= \int_V \; \mathcal{U}\, dV \end{aligned}\]

    Note: In (i, ii, iii), strain \(\epsilon\) is elastic strain for the recoverable stored elastic energy (in the elastic limit, total strain is elastic strain).

1D Small Strain Plasticity

For a case when the applied uniaxial stress \(\sigma\) is higher than the tensile yield strength (\(\sigma_Y\)) of the material \((\sigma > \sigma_Y\)), then \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon &= \epsilon^e + \epsilon^p; \quad \textrm{and}\\ \sigma &= E\, \epsilon^e \quad \textrm{(Hooke's Law)} \end{aligned}\] where \(\epsilon\) is the total strain, \(\epsilon^e\) and \(\epsilon^p\) are elastic and plastic strains, respectively. Hooke’s Law is for Linear Elastic Materials and relates elastic strain to stress.
Under elastic-plastic deformation, \[\begin{aligned} \textrm{Total work done per unit volume is} \quad \mathcal{W} &= \int_{0}^{\epsilon} \sigma\,d\epsilon\\ \textrm{Elastic energy stored per unit volume is} \quad \mathcal{U} &= \dfrac{1}{2}\sigma \epsilon^e \end{aligned}\]

Torsion

  1. Torsion of Cylindrical Elastic Bars
    Consider cylindrical coordinate system with position coordinates \((r,\theta, z)\) and basis vectors \(\{\bm{\hat{e}}_r, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\theta}, \bm{\hat{e}}_z \}\) for a hollow cylindrical bar that has inner radius \(R_i\), outer radius \(R_o\), length \(L\), and shear modulus \(G\). Consider \(\alpha = \frac{d\phi}{dz}\) as rotation/twist per unit length and angle in radians. \[\begin{aligned} \textrm{Total Twist} \quad \Phi &= \int_0^L \alpha \, dz \\ \textrm{For uniform geometry and material across length } \Phi &= \alpha\, L \\ \textrm{Shear Stress } \quad \tau &= \sigma_{\theta z} = G\,\gamma_{\theta z} = G\,\alpha\, r = \dfrac{T\,r}{J}\\ \textrm{Torsional Moment/Torque } \quad T &= GJ\alpha = GJ\frac{\Phi}{L}\\ J \textrm{ is polar moment of inertia with} \quad J &=\iint_A r^2\,dA =\frac{\pi (R_o^4-R_i^4)}{2} \;\; \end{aligned}\]

Thin Wall Pressurized Cylinders and Spheres

Consider an internal pressure \(P_i\) which is greater than the outer pressure \(P_o\) acting on a thin-walled hollow structure with thickness \(t << R \; \text{(radius)}\). Let us represent the difference between internal pressure (\(P_i\)) and outer pressure (\(P_o\)) as net pressure \(P\) so \(P = P_i - P_o\) (In the case when outer/external pressure is zero, then \(P=P_i\)).

  1. Thin Wall Cylinder
    Consider a cylindrical coordinate system with position coordinates \((r,\theta, z)\) and coordinate system basis \(\{\bm{\hat{e}}_r, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\theta}, \bm{\hat{e}}_z \}\) for a hollow cylinder that experiences net internal pressure, then \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{\theta\theta} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{t}\\ \sigma_{rr} &= 0 \qquad \qquad \qquad \quad (\textrm{note: } \sigma_{rr} \sim P << \sigma_{\theta\theta} \implies \sigma_{rr} \approx 0), \quad \textrm{and} \end{aligned}\] \[\begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \sigma_{zz} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{2 t} \qquad \qquad \text{(if the axial ends are closed/capped) \quad OR}\\ \sigma_{zz} &= 0 \qquad \qquad \quad \;\; \text{(if the axial ends are open) } \end{cases} \end{aligned}\]

    The material volume approximation for a thin-walled hollow cylinder with length \(L\) is \(V = 2 \pi R \, t \, L\).
    {Note: Volume of material \(V = \pi (R_o^2 - R_i^2) \,L = \pi (R_o+R_i)(R_o-R_i)\, L. \\ R_o - R_i = t\), and for a thin wall case, \(R_o+R_i \approx 2R \implies V \approx 2 \pi R \, t \, L\)}

  2. Thin Wall Sphere
    Consider a spherical coordinate system with position coordinates \((r,\theta, \phi)\) and coordinate system basis \(\{\bm{\hat{e}}_r, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\theta}, \bm{\hat{e}}_{\phi} \}\) for a hollow sphere that experiences net internal pressure, then \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{\theta\theta} = \sigma_{\phi\phi} &= \dfrac{P\,R}{2 t} \\ \sigma_{rr} &= 0 \qquad \qquad (\textrm{note: } \sigma_{rr} \sim P << \sigma_{\theta\theta} \implies \sigma_{rr} \approx 0) \end{aligned}\]

    The material volume approximation for a thin-walled sphere is \(V = 4 \pi R^2 \, t\).
    {Note: Volume of material \(V = \frac{4}{3} \pi (R_o^3 - R_i^3) = \frac{4}{3} \pi (R_o^2+R_i^2+R_oR_i)(R_o-R_i)\)
    with \(R_o - R_i = t\), and for a thin wall case, \(R_o \approx R_i \approx R\), leads to \(V \approx 4 \pi R^2 \, t\)}

Beam Bending

For a beam bending in the x-y plane, with x-axis along the neutral axis. At any given axial location \(x\) in the bent, slender beam:

General relations:

  1. Geometry relation \[\epsilon_{xx} = -\kappa y = -\frac{y}{\rho}\] Where \(y\) is the distance from the neutral axis, \(\kappa\) is the curvature of the local point, and \(\rho\) is the radius of curvature for the neutral axis.

  2. Moment balance \[M = -\iint_A y \, \sigma_{xx} \,dA\]

  3. Given \(v\) as deflection of the beam (at the neutral axis) in the transverse direction. \[\textrm{curvature: } \kappa = \frac{d^2 v}{d x^2},\qquad \textrm{slope: }\theta = \frac{dv}{dx}\]

For linear elastic materials:

  1. Moment-curvature relation \[M = \frac{EI}{\rho} = EI\kappa\]

  2. Axial stress and moment relation \[\sigma_{xx} = -\frac{My}{I}\]

  3. Governing equation for deflection \(v\) of a beam \[\dfrac{d^2 v}{dx^2} = \dfrac{M}{E I}\]

  4. Second moment of inertia for common beam cross-sections:
    For a beam with rectangular cross-section where the beam thickness is h (in the plane of bending) and width is b (width in z-direction if bending is in x-y plane), \[I = \frac{1}{12} bh^3\] For a cylindrical beam with cross-sectional outside radius \(R_o\) and internal radius \(R_i\), \[I = \frac{\pi}{4} (R_o^4-R_i^4)\]

Stress (General/3D) at a Point

For an orthogonal coordinate basis \(\bm{\hat{e}}_i\)

  1. Traction Vector (on a surface that is defined by its unit normal vector \(\bm{\hat{n}}\)): \[\textbf{t}(\bm{\hat{n}})=\lim_{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup A\to 0}\frac{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup\textbf{f}}{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup\textbf{A}}\]

  2. Stress Tensor: \[\sigma_{ij}=\bm{\hat{e}}_i \cdot \textbf{t}(\bm{\hat{e}}_j)\]

  3. Traction Vector from Stress Tensor: \[\textbf{t}(\bm{\hat{n}}) = \bm{\sigma} \,\bm{\hat{n}}\]

  4. Hydrostatic/Mechanical Pressure: \[\quad p = -\dfrac{1}{3}\, tr\, \bm{\sigma}\]

  5. Principal Stresses and Principal Directions

    • Principal stresses acting on a material point are the eigenvalues of the stress tensor at that material point. The directions of these principal stresses are given by the eigenvectors corresponding to each eigenvalue.

    • Maximum tensile stress at a material point is the maximum principal stress (when positive) at that material point.

  6. Equations of Motion/Cauchy Momentum Equations
    (In the Cartesian Coordinate System)

    \[\frac{\partial \sigma_{xx}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{xy}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{xz}}{\partial z} + b_x = \rho\frac{\partial^2 u_x}{\partial t^2}\] \[\frac{\partial \sigma_{xy}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{yy}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{yz}}{\partial z} + b_y = \rho\frac{\partial^2 u_y}{\partial t^2}\] \[\frac{\partial \sigma_{xz}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{yz}}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial \sigma_{zz}}{\partial z} + b_z = \rho\frac{\partial^2 u_z}{\partial t^2}\] (Equilibrium equations are obtained when there is no acceleration \({\ddot{\textbf{u}}}={\textbf{0}}\). The body is static.)

Second Order Tensor

For a second order tensor, in index notation, subscripts i and j represent values as either {1,2,3} or {x,y, z} in the cartesian coordinate system, represent {r, \(\theta\), z} in the cylindrical coordinate system, and {r, \(\theta, \phi\)} in the spherical coordinate system. With \(\bm{A}\) as a second order (3x3) tensor (it can represent a stress or a strain tensor),

  1. Transpose of a Tensor: \(\bm{A}^T\) \[[{A}_{ij}]^T = [{A}_{ji}]\]

  2. Trace of a Tensor: \[tr\, \textbf{A} = A_{xx} + A_{yy}+A_{zz}\]

  3. Deviatoric Part or Deviator of a Tensor: \[dev \, \textbf{A} = \textbf{A}_0 = \textbf{A} - \dfrac{1}{3}\, (tr \textbf{A})\, \bm{1}\]

  4. Determinant of a Tensor:
    det\(\textbf{A}\) is determinant of \(\textbf{A}\) matrix.

    Determinant of a \(3\times 3\) matrix: \[det \begin{bmatrix} A_{xx} & A_{xy} & A_{xz}\\ A_{yx} & A_{yy} & A_{yz}\\ A_{zx} & A_{zy} & A_{zz} \end{bmatrix} =A_{xx}\,(A_{yy}\,A_{zz} - A_{yz}\,A_{zy}) -A_{xy}\, (A_{yx}\,A_{zz} - A_{yz}\,A_{zx}) +A_{xz}\, (A_{yx}\,A_{zy}-A_{yy}\,A_{zx})\] Determinant of a \(2\times 2\) matrix:
    \(det \begin{bmatrix} A_{xx} & A_{xy} \\ A_{yx} & A_{yy} \\ \end{bmatrix} =A_{xx}A_{yy}-A_{xy}A_{yx}\\ \)

  5. Eigen Values (Principal Values) and Eigen Directions (Principal Directions)
    Eigen/Principal values are solution \(\omega_i\)’s of the equation: \[det[\bm{A}-\omega\,\bm{1}]=0 \qquad \textrm{(where $\bm{1}$ is the identity tensor)}\] \[\implies det \begin{bmatrix} A_{xx}-\omega & A_{xy} & A_{xz}\\ A_{xy} & A_{yy}-\omega &A_{yz}\\ A_{xz} & A_{yz} & A_{zz}-\omega \end{bmatrix} = 0\] Note that for stress and strain, the highest eigenvalue when positive is the maximum tensile stress/strain acting at that material point.
    If \(\bm{\hat{m}}\) is the eigenvector (Principal direction) corresponding to an eigenvalue (Principal value) \(\omega_i\), then: \[[\bm{A}-\omega_i\,\bm{1}]\,\{\bm{\hat{m}}\}=\{\textbf{0}\}\] \[\begin{bmatrix} A_{xx}-\omega_i & A_{xy} & A_{xz}\\ A_{xy} & A_{yy}-\omega_i &A_{yz}\\ A_{xz} & A_{yz} & A_{zz}-\omega_i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} m_x\\ m_y\\ m_z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\] \[\textrm{where } \quad m_x^2 + m_y^2 + m_z^2 = 1 \quad \textrm{since } \bm{\hat{m}} \textrm{ is a unit vector.}\]

Coordinate Transformation

3D Transformation (General): Given a second order tensor \(\bm{A}\) or a vector \(\bm{v}\) in coordinate system \(\{\bm{e}_x, \bm{e}_y, \bm{e}_z\}\). If \(\bm{A}\) or \(\bm{v}\) transform to become \(\bm{A}^*\) & \(\bm{v}^*\) for a different rotated coordinate system \(\{\bm{e}^*_x, \bm{e}^*_y, \bm{e}^*_z\}\) then we can use the following relations to obtain components of the transformed tensor: \[\begin{aligned} {{[\bm{A}^*]}_{}}_{3\text{x}3} &= {{[\bm{Q}]}_{}}_{3\text{x}3} \; {{[\bm{A}]}_{}}_{3\text{x}3} \; {{[\bm{Q}^T]}_{}}_{3\text{x}3} \\ {{[\bm{v}^*]}_{}}_{3\text{x}1} &= {{[\bm{Q}]}_{}}_{3\text{x}3} \; {{[\bm{v}]}_{}}_{3\text{x}1} \end{aligned}\] where the individual components of the rotation tensor \(\bm{Q}\) are obtained from \[Q_{ij} = \bm{e}^*_i \cdot \bm{e}_j\] 2D Transformation - Rotation in x-y plane (e.g., Plane Stress, Plane Strain): \[\begin{aligned} A^*_{xx} &= \dfrac{1}{2}\,(A_{xx} + A_{yy}) + \dfrac{1}{2}\,(A_{xx} - A_{yy})\, \cos 2\theta + A_{xy}\,\sin 2\theta,\\ A^*_{yy} &= \dfrac{1}{2}\,(A_{xx} + A_{yy}) - \dfrac{1}{2}\,(A_{xx} - A_{yy})\, \cos 2\theta - A_{xy}\,\sin 2\theta,\\ A^*_{xy} &= - \dfrac{1}{2}\,(A_{xx} - A_{yy})\, \sin 2\theta + A_{xy}\,\cos2\theta \end{aligned}\]

Small Strain (General/3D) at a Point

  1. Displacement
    If \(\bm{u} \; (= u_x\, \bm{\hat{e}}_x + u_y\, \bm{\hat{e}}_y + u_z\, \bm{\hat{e}}_z\)) is the displacement vector of a material point due to deformation,

  2. Normal Strains (In the Cartesian Coordinate System) \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon_{xx} &= \frac{\partial u_x}{\partial x} \\ \epsilon_{yy} &= \frac{\partial u_y}{\partial y} \\ \epsilon_{zz} &= \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial z} \end{aligned}\]

  3. Shear Strains (In the Cartesian Coordinate System) \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon_{xy} &= \epsilon_{yx} = \dfrac{1}{2}\,\Big(\frac{\partial u_x}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial u_y}{\partial x}\Big) = \dfrac{1}{2}\, \gamma_{xy}\\ \epsilon_{yz} &= \epsilon_{zy} = \dfrac{1}{2}\,\Big(\frac{\partial u_y}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial y}\Big) = \dfrac{1}{2}\, \gamma_{yz}\\ \epsilon_{xz} &= \epsilon_{zx} = \dfrac{1}{2}\,\Big(\frac{\partial u_x}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial x}\Big) = \dfrac{1}{2}\, \gamma_{xz} \end{aligned}\]

  4. Volumetric Strain \[\epsilon_V = \frac{V-V_0}{V_0} \approx tr \bm{\epsilon} = \epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} + \epsilon_{zz}\]

  5. Strain Gauge Rosettes for Planar 2D Strain Measurements:
    Consider that a strain gauge is identified with number classifier \(\beta\) where angle \(\theta_{\beta}\) is strain gauge’s orientation with respect to the \(x\)-axis. If the strain gauge measures strain value \(\epsilon^*_{\beta}\) along its longitudinal direction, we have \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon^*_{\beta} &= \dfrac{1}{2}\,(\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy}) + \dfrac{1}{2}\,(\epsilon_{xx} - \epsilon_{yy})\, \cos 2\theta_{\beta} + \epsilon_{xy}\,\sin 2\theta_{\beta} \end{aligned}\] If there are three strain gauges in a Rosette, then these strain gauges are identified as \(\beta = 1,2\) and \(3\). In a plane (2D), one will need three strain gauge measurements (\(\epsilon^*_1, \epsilon^*_2, \epsilon^*_3\)) to obtain the three unknown strains \(\epsilon_{xx}, \epsilon_{yy}, \epsilon_{xy}\) and fully define strain at a point.

Linear Elastic Stress-Strain Relations (Hooke’s Law) in 3D

  1. Stress-Strain Relations for Isotropic Materials
    Strains from stress: \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon_{xx} &= \frac{1}{E} \,[\sigma_{xx} - \nu(\sigma_{yy} + \sigma_{zz})]\\ \epsilon_{yy} &= \frac{1}{E} \,[\sigma_{yy} - \nu(\sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{zz})]\\ \epsilon_{zz} &= \frac{1}{E} \,[\sigma_{zz} - \nu(\sigma_{yy} + \sigma_{xx})]\\ \epsilon_{xy} &= \frac{1}{2G} \,\sigma_{xy} = \epsilon_{yx}\\ \epsilon_{yz} &= \frac{1}{2G} \,\sigma_{yz} = \epsilon_{zy}\\ \epsilon_{xz} &= \frac{1}{2G} \,\sigma_{xz} = \epsilon_{zx} \end{aligned}\]

    Stress from strains: \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_{xx} &= \lambda (\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} + \epsilon_{zz}) + 2G \epsilon_{xx}\\ \sigma_{yy} &= \lambda (\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} + \epsilon_{zz}) + 2G \epsilon_{yy}\\ \sigma_{zz} &= \lambda (\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} + \epsilon_{zz}) + 2G \epsilon_{zz}\\ \sigma_{xy} &= 2G\epsilon_{xy}\\ \sigma_{yz} &= 2G\epsilon_{yz}\\ \sigma_{xz} &= 2G\epsilon_{xz} \end{aligned}\]

  2. Stress-Strain Relations for Transversely Isotropic Materials
    If the material is transversely isotropic in the \(x-y\) plane, \[\begin{aligned} \epsilon_{xx} &= \dfrac{1}{E} \,\sigma_{xx} - \dfrac{\nu}{E} \,\sigma_{yy} - \dfrac{\nu'}{E'} \sigma_{zz}\\ \epsilon_{yy} &= \dfrac{1}{E} \,\sigma_{yy} - \dfrac{\nu}{E} \,\sigma_{xx} - \dfrac{\nu'}{E'} \sigma_{zz}\\ \epsilon_{zz} &= \dfrac{1}{E'} \,\sigma_{zz} - \dfrac{\nu'}{E} \,\sigma_{xx} - \dfrac{\nu'}{E} \sigma_{yy}\\ \epsilon_{xy} &= \frac{1}{2G} \,\sigma_{xy} = \epsilon_{yx}\\ \epsilon_{yz} &= \frac{1}{2G'} \,\sigma_{yz} = \epsilon_{zy}\\ \epsilon_{xz} &= \frac{1}{2G'} \,\sigma_{xz} = \epsilon_{zx} \end{aligned}\]

Selected Useful Relations between Elastic Constants
for Linear Isotropic Materials

With \(E\) as the Elastic/Young’s modulus and \(\nu\) as the Poison’s ratio,

\(G=\dfrac{E}{2(1+\nu)}\) \(K=\dfrac{E}{3(1-2\nu)}\) \(\lambda=\dfrac{E\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}\)

where the Shear modulus \(G\) is defined by: \(\quad \sigma_{xy} = G\, \gamma_{xy} = 2G\, \epsilon_{xy},\)

and the Bulk modulus \(K\) is defined by: \(K = -\dfrac{p}{\epsilon_V} = -\dfrac{p}{\frac{\triangle V}{V_0}}\quad\), where \(p\) is mechanical/hydrostatic pressure.
\(\lambda \;\) is Lameś constant.

Slender Column Buckling

Euler’s equation for slender column buckling: \[F_{crit} = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{c^2 L^2}\] Here \(F_{crit}\) is the lowest compressive load that can cause buckling, \(c\) is a buckling factor that depends on the boundary conditions, \(I\) is the second moment of inertia of the beam column, \(E\) is Young’s modulus, and L is the length of the column.
Fixed-Free \(c=2\), Pinned-Pinned \(c=1\), Fixed-Fixed \(c=\frac{1}{2}\), Fixed-Pinned \(c=0.7\).

von Mises Yield Criterion

At a material point, the elastic constitutive equation fails to hold when a yield condition is met. One of the prominent yield condition used is the Mises (named after von Mises) yield condition: \[\text{Yield occurs/starts at a material point when }\quad \bar{\sigma} = \sigma_Y\] \[\textrm{where,}\qquad \quad \bar{\sigma} = +\,\sqrt{\; \frac{1}{2}\Big\{(\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy})^2 + (\sigma_{yy} - \sigma_{zz})^2 + (\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{zz})^2\Big\} \; + \;3(\sigma_{xy}^2 + \sigma_{yz}^2 + \sigma_{xz}^2) \; }\] is the equivalent Mises stress, and \(\sigma_Y\) is the tensile yield strength (material property) of the material.

Fracture

Applied stress intensity factor (Mode I): \[K = \sigma^\infty Q \sqrt{\pi a}\] where \(\sigma^\infty\) is the far field stress (external stress), \(Q\) is the geometry and load factor, \(a\) is the characteristic crack length.
The criterion for fracture initiation or crack extension to not occur in response to slowly applied loading of monotonically increasing magnitude is taken as \(K < K_C\), where \(K_C\) is the material and thickness dependent critical value of K and is called the fracture toughness.
The brittle fracture happens when: \(K \ge K_C\)
(Note: Plane strain fracture toughness \({K_I}_c\) is a material property. It is a conservative method of defining fracture toughness, and \({K_I}_c\) is independent of thickness.)

Fatigue

  1. S-N Curve Method:
    The fatigue failure criteria for cyclic loading from multiple stress levels using Miner’s Rule is \[\sum_i \frac{N_i}{{N_f}_i} = 1\] Where \(N_i\) is the number of cycles that have already been run for the i\(^{th}\) stress magnitude, and \({N_f}_i\) is the number of cycles to failure corresponding to the i\(^{th}\) stress amplitude.

  2. Paris’ Law: \[\frac{da}{dN} = C\triangle K^m\]
    Integration of the Paris equation gives: \[\begin{aligned} N_f &= \frac{1}{C\,(\triangle \sigma \,Q \sqrt{\pi})^m} \, \frac{1}{(1-\frac{m}{2})}\, \Big\{a_f^{1-\frac{m}{2}} - a_i^{1-\frac{m}{2}}\Big\} \quad \text{ for } m \ne 2\\ &= \frac{1}{C\,(\triangle \sigma \,Q \sqrt{\pi})^2} \, \Big\{ \ln (\dfrac{a_f}{a_i}) \Big\} \qquad \quad \text{when } m = 2, \end{aligned}\] Where \(N_f\) is the number of cycles for a crack to grow from initial length \(a_i\) to a final length \(a_f\). In the equation, C and m are material properties, Q is the geometry and load factor, and \(\triangle \sigma\) is the magnitude of stress variation in one cycle.

Linear Viscoelasticity

  1. To model a material’s time/rate-dependent response, a viscoelastic material model has at least one or more springs (elastic response representation) and at least one or more dashpots (viscous response representation). The numbers and arrangements of the springs and dashpots will govern the viscoelastic model’s behavior.

  2. In linear viscoelasticity, both the elastic and viscous responses are assumed to be linear. The principle of superposition can be applied (since the model is linear).

  3. Individual spring \(i\) is modeled using Hooke’s elastic solid relation \(\sigma^{e^{(i)}} = E^{(i)}\, \epsilon^{e^{(i)}}\). Where \(\sigma^{e^{(i)}}, \epsilon^{e^{(i)}}\) are stress and strain acting on spring \(i\), and \(E^{(i)}\) is the elastic modulus of the spring \(i\).

  4. Individual dashpot \(i\) is modeled using Newtonian fluid model \(\sigma^{v^{(i)}} = \eta^{(i)} \, \dot{\epsilon}^{v^{(i)}}\). Where \(\sigma^{v^{(i)}} , \dot{\epsilon}^{v^{(i)}}\) are stress and strain-rate acting on the dashpot \(i\), and \(\eta^{(i)}\) is the viscosity of the dashpot \(i.\) Here \(\dot{()}\) represents the time derivative.

  5. A viscoelastic constitutive material model is a relationship between {\(\sigma, \epsilon, \dot{\sigma}, \dot{\epsilon}\)} (all or some of these) in terms of model parameters \(\eta\)’s and \(E\)’s. Where \(\sigma\) and \(\epsilon\) are the overall stress and strain in the viscoelastic material.
    For example, for a Maxwell Model where a spring and a dashpot are in series, the relationship is \[\dfrac{\dot{\sigma}}{E} + \dfrac{\sigma}{\eta} = \dot{\epsilon}\]

  6. Oscillatory Rheology:
    Provides frequency (and/or temperature) dependent measurements of \(G', G''\) and \(\tan\delta\) as viscoelastic material property parameters. \(G'\) is the Storage Modulus, \(G''\) is the Loss Modulus, and \(\tan\delta \; \Big( = \dfrac{G'' }{G'}\Big)\) is the Loss Tangent. \(\tan\delta\) represents mechanical damping.

  7. Time-Temperature Superposition (TTS): TTS is a principle used in linear viscoelasticity to predict the behavior of materials under different time and temperature conditions. It relies on the assumption that the effect of temperature on a viscoelastic material’s behavior can be represented as a shift in time (a higher temperature can accelerate the time effect). Temperature shift factor \(a_T(T)\) is used to create a master curve. The shift factor can be obtained using Arrhenius or WLF (Williams-Landel-Ferry) models.

  8. Integral Method:
    The application of the Boltzmann Superposition Principle gives integral relations.
    Stress response for a given strain history \(\epsilon(t)\): \[\sigma(t) = E_r(t)\,\epsilon_0 + \int_0^t E_r(t-\tau) \; \dot{\epsilon}(\tau) \, d\tau,\] where \(E_r(t)\) is the time-dependent stress relaxation Elastic Modulus.
    Strain response for a given stress history \(\sigma(t)\): \[\epsilon(t) = J_c(t)\, \sigma_0 + \int_0^t J_c(t-\tau) \; \dot{\sigma}(\tau) \, d\tau,\] where \(J_c(t)\) is the time-dependent creep response Compliance Modulus.

Large Deformation Stress and Strain

  1. 1D Stress and Strain
    Consider uniaxial tension or compression with \(L_0, A_0\) as the original axial length and cross-sectional area, respectively, and \(L, A\) as the deformed axial length and cross-sectional area. When strains are large (strains much greater than a few percent): (i) the change in deformed geometry cannot be ignored and needs to be accounted for, and (ii) the material response (relationship between stress and strain) typically becomes nonlinear. \[\begin{aligned} \textbf{Engineering/Nominal Strain and Stress:}& \quad \epsilon_{eng} =\frac{\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup L}{L_0}, \quad \sigma_{eng} = \frac{F}{A_0}\\ \textbf{Stretch Ratio:}& \quad \lambda =\frac{L}{L_0} = \frac{L_0+\mathop{}\!\mathbin\bigtriangleup L}{L_0} = 1 + \epsilon_{eng}\\ \textrm{True Strain:}& \quad \epsilon =\ln (1+\epsilon_{eng}) = \ln(\lambda) \\\\ \textbf{True Stress:}& \quad \sigma =\frac{F}{A}\\ \textrm{True Stress:}& \quad \sigma = \lambda\; \sigma_{eng} \end{aligned}\]

  2. 3D Nonlinear Elastic (Hyperelastic) Modeling

    • Kinematics: Displacement Gradient \(\textbf{H}\): \(H_{ij} = \dfrac{\partial u_i}{\partial X_j}\) (\(\textbf{u}\) is displacement vector)
      Deformation Gradient \(\textbf{F} = \textbf{H + 1}\). In principal coordinate frame \([\textbf{F}] = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0& \lambda_3 \end{bmatrix}\).

    • Strain Measures: Strain measures are mathematical constructs to measure normalized deformations. There are many accepted strain measures. All strain measures are functions of \((\textbf{F}^T \textbf{F})\).
      One common (basic) strain measure is Green Strain \(\textbf{E}\), where \[\textbf{E} = \dfrac{1}{2}(\textbf{F}^T \textbf{F} - \textbf{1}) = \dfrac{1}{2}(\textbf{H}+\textbf{H}^T+\textbf{H}^T \textbf{H})\] For small strains, \(\textbf{H}^T \textbf{H}\) can be neglected. Green Strain for small strains: \[\textbf{E} \equiv \bm{\epsilon} = \dfrac{1}{2}(\textbf{H}+\textbf{H}^T)\]

    • Constitutive Model: Defined as free energy density function
      \(\psi =\hat{\psi}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3, \textrm{model parameters})\), where \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2,\lambda_3\) are principal stretches.

    • Neo-Hookean Model (a simple nonlinear model): \(\psi = C_1(\lambda_1^2 + \lambda_2^2 + \lambda_3^2 -3 )\)

      where \(C_1\) is the material model parameter.
      Note: Ground state shear modulus \(G\) (initial shear modulus) \(\approx \frac{C_1}{2}\).

    • Stresses (for isotropic, incompressible elastic materials):
      \(\sigma_1 = \lambda_1 \, \dfrac{\partial \psi}{\partial \lambda_1} -P^*; \qquad \sigma_2 = \lambda_2 \, \dfrac{\partial \psi}{\partial \lambda_2} -P^*; \qquad \sigma_3 = \lambda_3 \, \dfrac{\partial \psi}{\partial \lambda_3} -P^*,\)
      where \(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3\) are principal stresses, and \(P^*\) is an unknown pressure-like variable (also known as a Lagrange multiplier) to be obtained by the boundary conditions of the problem.
      Note: Volume ratio \(\dfrac{V}{V_0} = det \, \textbf{F} = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 \lambda_3 = 1\) for incompressible materials.
